Chapter 01

Introduction: Why This Guide Exists

A story about biological clocks, NHS bureaucracy, ovulation sticks, and why no doctor should have to figure this out alone.

I have wanted to write about this for a while. Not just the policy and the pay, but the messy, emotional, deeply human experience that sits underneath all of it. Because before I became someone who understood qualifying weeks and occupational maternity pay, I was a 27-year-old doctor, four years married, standing at the edge of a decision I hadn't expected to feel so complicated.

I had spent years putting children off. There was always something else first; more countries to visit, more exams to pass, more life to live. I had ingested contraception almost daily for years without a second thought, grateful for the promise of a childless existence on my own terms. And then, somewhere between graduating medical school and starting my foundation years, something shifted. Unexpectedly, almost against my will, the idea of a family stopped being something I was deferring and started being something I wanted.

What I hadn't anticipated was how quickly the desire to start a family would run up against the rigid, unforgiving structure of the National Health Service foundation training programme; where even trying for a baby had to be carefully timed to meet eligibility requirements for maternity pay.

The Calculation I Had to Make

The decision to try for a baby wasn't just personal; it became a strategic one almost immediately. I quickly realised that if I wanted to receive meaningful maternity pay, timing was everything. To qualify for Occupational Maternity Pay (the full NHS package), I needed 12 months of continuous NHS service, meaning that by the time you reach week 29 of pregnancy (roughly 11 weeks before your due date), you are still continuously employed by the NHS. That clock had started ticking from my first day as an FY1.

For ease, this meant not getting pregnant before January in FY1, and not after January in FY2 (although at the time I hadn't appreciated that I could go on maternity leave without a job to return to and still get full maternity pay). Anything later and I would cross into a gap in employment, losing my OMP eligibility entirely. If I didn't secure a training contract after FY2, I could be out of NHS employment for up to a year, resetting my continuity. If I then got into training the following year, I might face a further wait before being eligible again. All of this against the backdrop of increasing competition for training places and the unpredictability of where in the country you might be sent.

Maternity pay mattered to me. It wasn't greed; it was the difference between financial security and genuine hardship during one of the most vulnerable periods of my life. So I made a plan.

The TTC Rabbit Hole

Inundated with all the planning and still unsure of exactly when my baby making window opened and closed, I did what any slightly anxious, type-A doctor would do; I gave myself a few months to conceive, knowing full well that it can take a year or more. What followed was something I suspect many women will recognise; a deep dive into the world of Trying to Conceive. Ovulation sticks. Cycle tracking. Supplements, CoQ10, vitamin D, folic acid, researched obsessively at midnight after long shifts. I went down the rabbit hole completely.

My first cycle ended in a chemical pregnancy. For those unfamiliar, this is an early pregnancy loss; a positive test, then gone, usually before week 5. It is technically common. Statistically unremarkable. And completely devastating. The week I was pregnant changed something in me. For those brief days I had started to imagine a future that felt suddenly, viscerally real. Losing it, even so early, was a grief I hadn't expected and didn't know how to hold.

💛

If This Resonates With You

Early pregnancy loss is far more common than it is spoken about, especially among women who are trying to conceive under pressure. If you have experienced this, you are not alone and your grief is valid; however brief the pregnancy was. The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk) offers support specifically for early losses.

The second month brought its own challenges. Ovulation windows were overshadowed by night shifts. I became, by my own admission, obsessive; which was not good for my mental health, my relationship, or my ability to be present at work. By the third month, my partner and I made a deliberate decision to stop trying. We had a long conversation about waiting, perhaps until training was secured, perhaps 3 to 5 years. We were young. We had time. We were lucky to be able to say that, and I knew it.

And then, the month we stopped trying, I got pregnant. Our twins were due on the 6th of December, three months into FY2.

Why I Wrote This

I was fortunate. My planning worked out, not because I had inside knowledge, but because I spent an enormous amount of time piecing together information from BMA documents, NHS Employers circulars, deanery FAQs, and HR departments who sometimes gave me contradictory answers. It was exhausting, and I was doing it while sleep-deprived, emotionally stretched, and trying to be a competent doctor at the same time.

This guide is what I wish had existed. It is not a dry policy document. It is a practical, honest walkthrough of everything you actually need to know; the pay system, the timing considerations, LTFT, returning to work, written by a doctor who has lived it. Whether your pregnancy is planned or unplanned, whether you are in FY1 or a specialty trainee, whether you are just beginning to think about this or already navigating it, I hope something here saves you hours of scrolling and a great deal of unnecessary anxiety.

You should not have to figure this out alone. No one should.

Chapter 02

Being Pregnant at Work

Your rights during pregnancy are broader than most doctors realise. Here is what you are entitled to from day one.

Your rights during pregnancy are broader than most doctors realise. From day one, you are protected in ways that are often poorly communicated, inconsistently applied, and frequently left out of medical training entirely.

Before we move into the complexities of maternity pay later in this book, it is important to start here: what it actually means to be pregnant while working in the NHS. This is not just a period of rota adjustments and clinic appointments. It is a transition into a new phase of your life where you will quickly realise the importance of advocating for yourself; not only for your own wellbeing, but for your baby or babies as well.

At times, it can feel like you are constantly navigating two competing responsibilities: being a safe, reliable doctor and adjusting to a changing body, energy level, and set of needs. The reality is that both matter. Learning to protect your health within the system is not optional; it is part of safe practice, even if it is not always treated that way.

Antenatal Appointments: You Don't Need to Use Annual Leave

This is one of the most commonly misunderstood entitlements in medicine. You have a legal right to paid time off for all antenatal care, and that covers more than just your midwife and scan appointments. It also includes any antenatal or parenting classes that have been recommended by your doctor or midwife, so if your midwife suggests an antenatal course, attending it is a protected right, not something you need to fit around annual leave.

Your partner has a statutory right to unpaid time off to accompany you to up to two antenatal appointments. If your partner also works for the NHS, they may be entitled to paid time off; it is worth them checking their own trust's policy directly.

Telling Your Employer: The Risk Assessment

When you decide to tell your employer you are pregnant, your line manager (usually your clinical or educational supervisor) is legally required to conduct a pregnancy risk assessment. The purpose is to identify any health and safety concerns in your working environment and any adjustments your working pattern may need. Common examples in medicine include:

  • Exposure to radiation (for example, portable X-rays or fluoroscopy)
  • Exposure to certain infections or high-risk clinical areas
  • Night shifts and on-call duties
  • Physically demanding roles, such as manual handling or prolonged standing
  • High-intensity environments where fatigue may increase risk

You may not need any adjustments initially, and that is fine. But you are entitled to request a repeat risk assessment at any point during your pregnancy if your needs change. Common adjustments doctors negotiate include coming off nights, being removed from on-call rotas, transitioning to more desk-based or outpatient work, or reducing overall working percentage temporarily.

When Adjustments Become Essential

In some cases, adjustments are not optional or gradual. They become necessary for safety.

🩺 Personal Experience: Pelvic Girdle Pain at 32 Weeks

For me, this became clear around 32 weeks of pregnancy, when I developed severe pelvic girdle pain. My mobility became significantly restricted to the point where walking on a ward round was difficult, and prolonged standing was no longer feasible.

At the time, I was working on a ward where I was the only doctor covering approximately 30 patients, with multiple consultant-led ward rounds throughout the day. The combination of physical strain, workload intensity, and reduced mobility made the situation increasingly unsafe, both clinically and personally.

There was a point where the impact of fatigue and physical limitation became very real. After a particularly demanding shift, I was involved in a minor car incident, a clear signal that I was no longer functioning safely under the conditions I was working in. My legs were heavy and fatigued, and my overall capacity to safely manage both driving and clinical responsibility was compromised.

This is an important point that is often overlooked in healthcare culture: pregnancy-related symptoms do not only affect comfort; they can directly affect safety, clinical judgement, and functional capacity. Following review, I was signed off by my GP and removed completely from the rota, transitioning to remote clinical duties in line with occupational health advice. This adjustment was not about stepping away from responsibility. It was about recognising that safe practice changes during pregnancy, and that protecting both patient safety and personal health are not competing priorities. They are aligned.

⚠️

Your Pay Cannot Be Reduced for Adjustments

If your working pattern is adjusted during pregnancy, your pay must remain the same. If your department cannot accommodate suitable alternative work, you must be suspended on full pay. This is a legal requirement under the NHS Terms and Conditions of Service (section 15.34).

The Reality of Guilt and Advocacy

Although these rights exist clearly in policy, many doctors experience a very different reality in practice. You may feel pressure to minimise disruption, avoid raising concerns, or push through. This is common, but it is not a requirement of your role.

Pregnancy often forces an uncomfortable shift: learning to advocate for yourself in environments where you are used to prioritising patients, colleagues, and service demands over your own needs. This is not selfishness. It is responsibility; for your health and for your pregnancy.

Sickness During Pregnancy

Sickness during pregnancy is treated differently from standard sickness absence:

  • Any sickness prior to the last 4 weeks before your due date is treated as normal sickness leave and paid according to the NHS sick leave policy based on your years of service.
  • Isolated pregnancy-related sick days within the final 4 weeks before birth are disregarded if you choose to continue working until your planned maternity leave start date.
  • If pregnancy-related illness runs into the 4 weeks before birth, your maternity leave will start automatically from that point, regardless of your intended start date.
ℹ️

Pregnancy Loss Before 25 Weeks

If you suffer a pregnancy loss before 25 weeks, it is classified as a miscarriage under section 15.33 of the NHS Terms and Conditions of Service, and normal sickness absence provisions apply. This does not mean it is treated dismissively; it means your sick leave protections apply. You are entitled to time off to recover, and your employer should treat this with sensitivity. If you feel you need more time or support than sick leave allows, speak to your occupational health department or your educational supervisor.

If You Are Due to Rotate Trusts Late in Pregnancy

This is a situation many rotational trainees worry about and very few people know the answer to. If you are due to rotate to a new trust after week 29 of pregnancy, you are entitled to choose to remain at your current trust until you give birth, with your contract extended appropriately. You do not have to rotate on your scheduled date if doing so would be clinically or practically unreasonable at that stage of pregnancy. This is set out in section 15.83 of the NHS Employers Terms and Conditions of Service handbook.

Chapter 03

The NHS Maternity Pay System Demystified

Two types of pay. Very different rules. Understanding both is essential.

NHS maternity pay has two distinct components; many doctors mistakenly assume they're either entitled to both or neither. In reality, your eligibility for each depends on completely different criteria.

Occupational Maternity Pay (OMP)

This is the enhanced NHS pay, the "good stuff." It's your employer's contractual maternity pay on top of any statutory entitlement. Under the 2016 Terms and Conditions:

PeriodPay LevelNotes
Weeks 1–8Full payIncludes basic salary + applicable supplements
Weeks 9–26Half pay + SMP (if eligible)Together these should not exceed full pay
Weeks 27–39SMP only (if eligible)Statutory amount; see below
Weeks 40–52UnpaidUnless your trust offers additional support

Eligibility for OMP

To qualify for OMP, by the start of the 11th week before your Expected Week of Childbirth (EWC), around 29 weeks pregnant, you must:

  • Still be employed by the NHS (your contract must be active at this point)
  • Have completed 12 months of continuous NHS service across any NHS employers, not just your current trust
  • Intend to return to NHS work for 13 weeks after maternity leave ends, with any NHS employer
🤔

Wait, What?

If your fixed-term contract expires during maternity leave, you may not have to return to that trust at all, and you could still keep your OMP. The return obligation applies to any NHS employer, not your current one. A trust grade post, clinical fellow role, or training contract elsewhere all count. More on this in Chapter 6.

Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP)

SMP is the government-funded statutory minimum. It is paid for up to 39 weeks and has different eligibility rules from OMP; this is where it can feel complicated for rotational doctors.

PeriodRate
First 6 weeks90% of your average weekly earnings
Weeks 7–39Standard SMP rate (£194.32/week in 2026; check gov.uk as it is updated each April)

To qualify for SMP, you usually need 26 weeks of continuous service with the same employer by the qualifying week. For many professions that is straightforward. For rotational trainees, it is not always so simple.

SMP and Rotational Training: the Trust Continuity Problem

Resident doctors often rotate trusts every 4–6 months, which naturally raises the question: "If I changed trust recently, have I lost my SMP?" The good news is, not necessarily.

ℹ️

The Lead Employer Exception

Some deaneries use a "lead employer" model where a single trust remains your legal employer throughout training, even while you rotate hospitals. If this applies to you, continuity is usually preserved and SMP becomes much simpler. It is worth checking with your payroll, HR, or deanery early, rather than assuming.

✅

2026 Reality: Trainees Have Extra Protection

Even without a Lead Employer arrangement, doctors in training often have additional protection. NHS Employers guidance states that if a trainee changes employer solely because of a required training rotation and loses SMP continuity as a result, the current employer should pay the equivalent value of SMP that would otherwise have been received. So while rotational training can make the paperwork messy, it does not automatically mean you lose maternity pay. This is a really important point that many doctors are not aware of.

A Note on Due Dates: Don't Cut It Fine

Your early due date is estimated from your last menstrual period (LMP), but your official due date often changes after the 12-week dating scan, sometimes by only a few days. Those few days can matter more than you'd think. A revised due date can shift your qualifying week, your OMP eligibility date, and when key forms need submitting.

What if You Don't Qualify for OMP or SMP?

You may still be entitled to Maternity Allowance (MA), a government benefit paid through HMRC. MA is worth knowing about for another reason too: unlike SMP, which is treated as income and taxed accordingly, Maternity Allowance is a non-taxable benefit. In some circumstances this means that MA can actually leave you slightly better off in take-home terms than an equivalent SMP payment, even though the headline rates look similar. It is worth checking your individual situation, particularly if you are close to a tax band threshold. For full eligibility details and to apply, visit gov.uk/maternity-allowance.

You are always entitled to take maternity leave regardless of your pay entitlement. Never let a pay concern stop you taking your full 52 weeks.

The Money Reality: What You Actually Stand to Lose

Abstract rules are hard to act on. Numbers are not. Here is a rough illustration using approximate figures for a foundation/core trainee in England with a basic salary of £38,831 per year and the 2026 SMP rate of £194.32 per week (figures vary by grade and trust; check your own payslip):

❌ SMP Only (OMP missed)

Weeks 1–6: ~90% average weekly earnings (~£671/week)

Weeks 7–39: £194.32/week statutory rate

Weeks 40–52: Unpaid

Approximate total for 52 weeks: ~£10,000–£11,000

✅ Full OMP + SMP

Weeks 1–8: Full pay (~£746/week)

Weeks 9–26: Half pay + SMP (~£567/week)

Weeks 27–39: SMP only (~£194.32/week)

Weeks 40–52: Unpaid

Approximate total for 52 weeks: ~£18,700

💰

The Difference Can Be Around £8,000

Missing one qualifying date, failing to push back on an incorrect HR decision, or not understanding your continuous service rights can cost you the difference between OMP and SMP; around £8,000 on a foundation/core trainee salary. That is not a small administrative error. It is a significant financial loss that is entirely avoidable with the right information.

Adoption Leave

If you are growing your family through adoption, your entitlements are very closely mirrored to maternity leave. Here is a quick overview:

EntitlementDetail
Total leave52 weeks, plus accrued annual leave
Occupational Adoption Leave pay8 weeks full pay, 18 weeks half pay, 13 weeks statutory adoption pay, 13 weeks unpaid
Notice to change return date28 days
Pay calculationBased on 2 payslips preceding the matching week (the week the adoption agency notifies your employer you have been matched with a child)

If you are fostering, you can choose to start adoption leave when the placement begins. Any fostering allowances you receive will not affect your adoption leave pay. If the adoption breaks down after it has begun, you are entitled to continue your adoption leave until your original planned end date and continue to receive adoption pay as originally planned. If you are adopting from overseas, specific notification rules apply; the key requirement is giving your trust 28 days notice of the child's arrival date once you receive official permission to adopt.

  • SMP: Statutory pay; requires 26 weeks with the same employer by the qualifying week. Rotational trainee protections may still mean you receive the equivalent payment.
  • Maternity Allowance: Available if you don't qualify for SMP. Unlike SMP, it is not taxed.
  • Either way: You can always take the leave. Never sacrifice your 52-week entitlement due to pay uncertainty.

    The Qualifying Week: Why Timing Matters More Than You Think

    One week can be the difference between receiving full NHS maternity pay or losing thousands of pounds.

    Now that you understand the different types of maternity pay, it's now time to focus on one of the most important moments in the entire maternity pay process: the qualifying week. One week that determines several crucial dates.

    💡

    A Quick Disclaimer

    This is not about trying to plan pregnancy with military precision. Life does not work that way, and many things are outside our control. But if timing is something you are able to consider, understanding the rules now could save you stress, confusion, and a substantial amount of money later.

    The Two Key Qualifying Dates

    EntitlementWhat's AssessedAt What Point?
    OMP12 months NHS serviceBy start of 11th week before EWC (~29 weeks pregnant)
    SMP eligibility26 weeks with current employerBy the qualifying week (15th week before EWC, ~25 weeks pregnant)
    SMP pay rateAverage weekly earnings8-week window ending at approximately week 25 of pregnancy
    🔑

    The SMP Pay Calculation Window

    Your SMP rate is based on your average earnings in the 8-week period ending at approximately week 25 of pregnancy. What you earn in the weeks leading up to this point can affect what you are paid for many months afterwards. The following may all increase your SMP calculation if they appear in your payslips during this window: basic salary increases, on-call supplements, nodal pay uplifts, additional rostered enhancements, locum or bank work paid through payroll, and pay progression after ARCP. When calculating OMP, include payslips from all trusts and ensure annual leave payments are also included.

    How Getting Pregnant at Different Times Affects Your Entitlement

    ❌ Less Favourable Scenario

    You conceive around 2 months into your first NHS post. By 29 weeks pregnant, you will have approximately 9 months of NHS service. You need 12 months. You are 3 months short, you do not qualify for OMP, and fall back on SMP only; a substantial reduction in income during maternity leave.

    ✅ More Favourable Scenario

    You conceive around 7 months after starting NHS employment. By the time you reach the OMP eligibility point, you are much more likely to have completed your required 12 months NHS service. Result: 8 weeks full pay + 18 weeks half pay + SMP; a significantly stronger overall package.

    Practical Timing Considerations

    • When does your 12-month NHS anniversary fall? This is often the biggest milestone for OMP.
    • Are you rotating to a new trust soon? A trust change during weeks 17–25 of pregnancy could affect your SMP calculation, mainly due to errors and trusts not communicating with each other. Check your lead employer situation early.
    • Are you expecting a pay band change or pay rise? For example, ARCP progression, moving up nodal pay scales, or a new rota supplement. If these changes happen before the earnings calculation window, they may improve your SMP. Importantly, even if your ARCP outcome occurs outside the 8-week calculation window, the NHS Terms and Conditions (section 15) state that where a doctor receives an ARCP outcome prior to commencing leave, or during leave, which would have enabled progression to the next training grade and a move to a higher nodal point, that higher nodal point must be reflected in their maternity pay. So an ARCP progression will be factored in regardless of when it falls.
    • Are you planning locum shifts? Locum earnings paid via your main trust payslips (not a separate agency) can increase your SMP rate during the calculation window.
    • Are you planning unpaid leave during weeks 17–25? If unpaid leave falls within the 8-week SMP calculation window, those weeks of zero income will be included in your average. This can meaningfully reduce your SMP rate even if your salary is otherwise good. Time any unpaid gaps outside this window wherever possible.
    ℹ️

    If You Were on Sick Leave During the Calculation Window (Section 15.23)

    If you were on unpaid sick leave, or on sick leave attracting only half pay, during the 8-week earnings calculation window, your SMP could be unfairly reduced. Under section 15.23 of the NHS Terms and Conditions of Service, your average weekly earnings for any period of sick absence in that window should be calculated as if you had received your full notional sick pay, in line with sections 14.4 and 14.5. In plain terms: if sickness affected your pay during that period, you should not be penalised in your SMP calculation. Raise this with HR or payroll if it applies to you.

    💡

    Don't Overthink It

    Timing a pregnancy around NHS pay rules is not something everyone wants to, or is able to, do. Life rarely runs to a perfect spreadsheet, and that is completely okay. This chapter is not about telling you when to have a baby. It is simply about giving you the information many doctors wish they had earlier, so you can make informed decisions if timing happens to be relevant to you.

    Understanding Continuous NHS Service: It Is Broader Than You Think

    One of the most common sources of confusion and missed entitlement is a misunderstanding of what counts as continuous NHS service. Many doctors assume it means unbroken time with a single trust. It does not. Continuous service spans the whole of your NHS career, across different employers, and certain breaks do not reset the clock.

    NHS employers for these purposes include trusts, boards, health authorities, and the Northern Ireland Health Service. Time worked across any of these counts cumulatively towards your 12-month OMP eligibility threshold.

    Breaks That Do Not Usually Break Continuity

    • A gap of 3 months or less between NHS jobs (the gap itself doesn't count as service, but continuity is usually maintained)
    • Time working under an honorary contract
    • Up to 12 months as a GP locum
    • Up to 12 months overseas on an approved postgraduate training programme
    • Up to 12 months of voluntary overseas service with a recognised international relief organisation
    • An approved career break or employment break scheme under NHS terms and conditions
    • Previous maternity, paternity, or shared parental leave, whether paid or unpaid
    • Time as a GP trainee under an approved trainee practitioner scheme

    Extra Rules for Resident Doctors on the 2016 Contract

    • Approved Out of Programme (OOP) placements outside the NHS may be disregarded when assessing continuous service
    • Time working in the health service of a UK Crown Dependency as part of an approved training programme may also be disregarded
    ℹ️

    Important Nuances

    Some breaks preserve continuity but do not add extra service time. Employers may also choose, at their discretion, to recognise other previous NHS or external service. Trusts can extend certain time limits beyond the defaults listed here. This means the rules work in your favour more often than HR may initially suggest. Always ask payroll or HR to confirm your specific employment history in writing before accepting any decision about your eligibility.

    Real-Life Scenarios

    🩺 Real-Life Scenario A: Qualifying Week Spanning Two Trusts

    A doctor's maternity pay qualifying weeks crossed over when she moved to a new trust in February, having over 12 months of continuous NHS service. She received two payslips for February; one from her new trust (slightly less than normal due to the start date) and a few days' pay plus annual leave owed from her old trust.

    Will both payslips be included in the SMP calculation? Yes. Both payslips from the qualifying period should be included, regardless of which trust issued them. The annual leave payment from the old trust should also be included. This is worth raising explicitly with HR at your new trust, as it requires coordination between two payroll teams who do not always communicate automatically. Put it in writing and ask them to confirm in writing that both payslips will be factored into the calculation.

    🩺 Real-Life Scenario B: Training Ending Near EDD (GPST3)

    A GPST3 doctor approaching the end of her training found out she was 7 weeks pregnant, with an expected due date falling a couple of months after her CCT date.

    1. What happens to her contract if training ends before the birth? This is where training status and employment status need to be separated. If a GP trainee has already triggered maternity entitlement while employed in training, the fixed-term training contract is normally extended for the purpose of maternity leave and maternity rights, rather than ending on the original CCT or rotation date. In practice, this means the contract does not simply terminate on the planned training end date; it is typically continued to allow access to maternity leave and any applicable maternity pay. She may therefore reach her educational CCT milestone and become a fully qualified GP during maternity leave, while remaining employed under the extended training contract for the duration of that leave. These are two separate timelines: the CCT date is an educational milestone, and the employment contract for maternity purposes is an employment protection and pay entitlement. Once maternity leave ends, the training contract would usually then come to a close unless another substantive role has been arranged.

    2. What if she gives birth a week after her EDD? Entitlement to maternity pay is based on the expected week of childbirth, not the actual birth date. Provided the qualifying week falls at a point when she still has 12 months' continuous NHS service, pay entitlement is unaffected by a late birth. She does not need to return to the same trust or practice after leave and can apply for a placement in a different practice or deanery. The BMA also notes: "If there is no right to return to be exercised because the contract would have ended if pregnancy and childbirth had not occurred, the repayment provisions will not apply." This means if the training contract would have ended regardless, she is not required to repay OMP on the basis of that expired contract.

    🩺 Real-Life Scenario C: Moving From Registrar to Consultant While Pregnant

    A doctor finishing her final year of specialty training has a consultant post lined up for the summer. She wants a short unpaid gap of three to four weeks between the two jobs. She is also pregnant.

    Continuous NHS service: A short gap between two substantive NHS posts does not necessarily break the continuity that matters for OMP eligibility, particularly if it is a planned transition. Whether continuity is preserved depends on how the gap is categorised; confirm in writing with both trusts before anything is signed.

    The SMP earnings risk: SMP is calculated by looking at what you actually earned during a specific 8-week window around week 25 of pregnancy. If those three or four weeks of unpaid leave fall inside that reference window, weeks of zero income will be included in the average, producing a meaningfully lower SMP rate, even if the new consultant salary is substantially higher. The fix: time the gap so it sits outside weeks 17 to 25 of pregnancy, or structure the transition differently. Speak to a BMA adviser before confirming your start dates with either employer.

    🎯 Chapter Summary

    • The qualifying week is the 15th week before your EWC. OMP eligibility is assessed at the 11th week before EWC.
    • Your SMP rate is based on average earnings in the 8 weeks ending around week 25 of pregnancy.
    • Unpaid leave, trust changes, and pay rises can all affect your calculation, positively or negatively.
    • Continuous NHS service spans all NHS employers; a trust change does not reset the clock.
    • Always verify your specific history in writing with HR before accepting any decision about eligibility.
    Chapter 05

    Maximising Your Maternity Pay: Get That Money, Honey

    Things you can actively do (and not do) to protect and increase your entitlement.

    1. Notify Your Employer at the Right Time

    You must notify your employer that you're pregnant and your intended start date for maternity leave by the end of the 15th week before your EWC. Earlier notification is always better, as it triggers your employer's obligation to act and prevents any delays.

    📋

    What to Submit

    You'll need: a MATB1 form (given by your midwife or GP after week 20), a letter stating your intended start date, and confirmation of your intended return date (provisional is fine). Get everything in writing and keep copies of all correspondence.

    2. Check Your Locum Earnings Strategy

    If you do locum shifts that are paid via your main trust payslips (as opposed to a separate agency), those earnings are included in your SMP calculation window. Doing additional shifts during weeks 17–25 of pregnancy will increase your average weekly earnings and therefore your SMP rate during weeks 1–6 of maternity leave (the 90% rate).

    ⚠️

    Travel Expenses Don't Count

    Reimbursed expenses, including mileage and travel allowances, are excluded from the SMP earnings calculation. Only your gross pay is counted. Don't confuse the two when estimating your SMP rate.

    2b. You Can Receive Maternity Pay From Two Employers

    This is one of the least known and most valuable things in this chapter. If you have worked for more than one employer during your qualifying period; for example, through agency or umbrella company locum work alongside your NHS role. You may be entitled to receive maternity pay from both simultaneously. SMP is employer-specific; each employer who meets the eligibility threshold owes you their own SMP calculation independently.

    This applies even if one of those employers is a locum agency paying you through an umbrella company. Provided you meet the qualifying criteria with that employer: 26 weeks of continuous service and average weekly earnings above the lower earnings limit. They are legally required to pay SMP. Many agencies will push back on this or claim it does not apply. It does. You may need to be persistent.

    ℹ️

    Which SMP Rate Applies If the Rate Changes Near Your Due Date?

    SMP rates are updated each April. The rate that applies to you is determined by when your maternity pay period begins, not when your baby is due. If your maternity leave starts before the April uprating, you receive the lower rate for those first weeks; if it starts after, you receive the higher rate. If your leave spans the April change, the new rate applies from the date it comes into effect. Always check the current rate at gov.uk and ask HR to confirm which rate has been applied to your calculation; errors here are common and worth catching early.

    🩺 Real-Life Scenario: NHS Educational Clinical Fellow and Maternity Pay From Two Sources

    A doctor working as an NHS educational clinical fellow on a fixed-term contract found herself navigating one of the more complex maternity pay situations; and came out with pay from two separate employers.

    Her employment history: She started NHS work in 2021, took an F3 year locuming in 2023, then became a clinical fellow in 2024 on a contract running to July 2026. Her baby was due in April 2026. Because of the F3 locuming year, her continuous NHS service was disrupted, which added an additional layer of complexity to her entitlement.

    Step 1: Informing her employer: She told her line manager at 18 weeks. He completed the paperwork and informed HR. A smooth start, though as she would discover, the complexity came later.

    Step 2: Continuous service: Her NHS service had started in 2021. The F3 locuming year did reset her continuous service clock; however, by the time her qualifying week arrived, she still had well over 12 months of continuous NHS service from her return to employment. This meant she remained eligible for OMP, despite the earlier gap.

    Step 3: The SMP rate question: Her due date was in April 2026, the same month SMP rates increased. She contacted HR proactively to ask which rate would apply and to ensure the amendment was made. This is exactly the right move; trusts do not always update calculations automatically when rates change, and it is worth confirming in writing.

    Step 4: SMP from her locum agency: Alongside her clinical fellow role, she had locumed through an agency under an umbrella company, paid as PAYE. Because she had been paid through payroll and met the qualifying criteria, she was entitled to SMP from the agency as a second, separate entitlement. The agency initially resisted. She pushed back, citing her legal entitlement. She was right, and eventually received SMP from both employers simultaneously.

    💡

    Key Lessons From This Scenario

    Tell your line manager early and follow up in writing. Check which SMP rate applies if your leave spans an April uprating. If you have locumed through an umbrella company as PAYE, you may be entitled to SMP from that employer too, even if they say otherwise. Keep records of all correspondence and be prepared to be persistent. You are entitled to what the law says you are entitled to.

    3. Accrue Your Annual Leave

    Annual leave continues to accrue during all 52 weeks of maternity leave, including the unpaid period. How much you accrue depends on your length of NHS service:

    • Under 5 years NHS service: you accrue 27 days per year
    • Over 5 years NHS service: you accrue 32 days per year
    • In addition, you accrue 8 bank holidays per year regardless of service length

    This means that if you take a full year of maternity leave, you could return with 35 to 40 days of paid leave to use. Plan to arrange these with the HR coordinator of your new rotation before you return. Note also that annual leave continues to accrue during any annual leave you take on return; so if you use accrued leave directly after maternity leave, you are still accruing further leave during that period.

    4. Understand the 13-Week Return Rule

    OMP is paid on the basis that you intend to return to NHS employment for at least 13 weeks after maternity leave. This is more flexible than many doctors realise:

    • The return can be with any NHS employer, not necessarily your current trust
    • Annual leave taken immediately on return counts towards the 13 weeks
    • Part-time and LTFT work counts; you do not need to return full-time
    • If your fixed-term training contract ends during leave, you may not need to return to that specific trust

    Roles that may satisfy the 13-week return requirement include: trust grade doctor, clinical fellow, locum bank contract (depends on trust wording), GP training post, specialty training post, or another substantive NHS role. If you are unsure whether your specific situation qualifies, ask HR in writing before your leave starts.

    ⚠️

    If Your Contract Ends During Leave

    Ask HR in writing before leave starts: "If my contract expires during maternity leave, do I need to repay OMP? Can return to another NHS employer satisfy the 13 weeks? What evidence would you need?" Getting this confirmed in advance avoids any nasty surprises on return. Remember: the BMA state that "If there is no right to return to be exercised because the contract would have ended if pregnancy and childbirth had not occurred, the repayment provisions will not apply."

    5. Medical Defence Organisation and GMC: Don't Miss This

    Most MDOs (MDU, MPS, MDDUS) treat maternity leave as a career break and do not require you to pay full subscriptions during this period. Many doctors continue paying unnecessarily; over a full year this is hundreds of pounds wasted. Contact your MDO before going on leave to apply for a career break discount or suspension, and remember to reactivate before your first clinical shift on return.

    Similarly, the GMC offers reduced registration fees during periods of non-practice. Write to them before your leave starts to apply; it is a simple process and worth doing.

    6. Check Your Salary Sacrifice Arrangements Before Week 17

    Many doctors participate in salary sacrifice schemes without giving them much thought: cycle to work, pension contributions, childcare arrangements. But salary sacrifice works by reducing your gross pay, and it is your gross pay that forms the basis of your SMP calculation. If your sacrificed salary is being deducted during the 8-week earnings window around week 25 of pregnancy, your average weekly earnings will look lower than they actually are, which means a lower SMP rate. Suspending the sacrifice temporarily before that window opens is a simple step that could meaningfully improve what you receive. Speak to your payroll team before making any changes, as the process varies by trust.

    7. Ask for Your Maternity Exemption Card

    The moment your midwife confirms your pregnancy, ask them to complete a FW8 form so you can apply for your Maternity Exemption certificate. This entitles you to free NHS prescriptions and free NHS dental treatment from the point your pregnancy is confirmed right through to 12 months after your baby is born.

    8. KIT Day Arbitrage: Timing Is Everything

    Most doctors know they have 10 Keeping In Touch days during maternity leave. Far fewer know that when you use them makes a significant financial difference.

    A KIT day is paid at your full basic daily rate regardless of when in your leave you use it. But during the first 26 weeks of maternity leave, you are already receiving either full or half pay. A KIT day taken during that period simply tops up pay you are largely already receiving, so the financial benefit is minimal. Save your KIT days for the nil-pay period, months 9 to 12 of your leave, when you are receiving nothing. Ten KIT days in the nil-pay period could add over two weeks' worth of income at a time when your bank account needs it most.

    ℹ️

    KIT Day Rules to Know

    KIT days cannot be used during the two weeks of compulsory maternity leave immediately after birth. They can be taken at any other point during maternity, parental, or adoption leave. They cannot be taken during your accrued annual leave period once parental leave has officially ended. Both you and your employer must agree to each KIT day in advance; never attend without written confirmation.

    ⚠️

    Indemnity: Check Before You Work

    If you are using KIT days or SPLiT days to do any clinical work, contact your medical defence organisation before you attend. Your standard membership may be suspended during leave, and you need to confirm you have the appropriate cover in place before you see a single patient.

    🤱

    If You Are Breastfeeding on a KIT Day

    Let your employer know in advance. Before you work a KIT day while breastfeeding, your employer is required to carry out a risk assessment and ensure appropriate facilities are available, including a private space to express milk that is not a toilet, somewhere to store expressed milk, and more frequent rest breaks.

    🎯 Key Action Points

    • Submit your MATB1 and notification letter before week 25 of pregnancy
    • Check your deanery's lead employer arrangement to protect SMP continuity
    • Check how locum shifts are paid; those on your main payslip affect SMP calculations
    • Ask HR to confirm how any pay rises or band changes will affect your maternity pay calculation
    • Suspend any salary sacrifice schemes before week 17 to avoid reducing your SMP earnings average
    • Contact your MDO to suspend membership before your leave starts; reactivate before your first clinical shift
    • Write to the GMC to apply for reduced fees during non-practice
    • Save your KIT days for the nil-pay period (months 9–12) for maximum benefit
    • If taking unpaid leave between NHS posts, check it falls outside your SMP calculation window

      Chapter 09

      When Things Don't Go to Plan: Special Circumstances

      Now that you understand the NHS pay system and how to financially protect yourself, it is worth knowing that some circumstances come with an additional level of support and protection. This chapter covers what happens when pregnancy does not follow the expected path.

      Preterm Births

      If your baby is born before 29 weeks, the rules around when your maternity leave starts differ slightly:

      • If you are still working when your baby is born early, your maternity leave starts on the first day of your absence.
      • If you are already on certified sick leave, it starts on the day of the birth.
      • Crucially, if your baby is born before 29 weeks and is in hospital, you have the option to split your maternity leave; you can take a minimum of 2 weeks immediately after the birth, and then pause your leave while your baby remains in hospital, resuming it when your baby is discharged. This means you can be at home with your baby during the hardest weeks, rather than counting down your leave while they are still in neonatal care.

      Neonatal Care Pay and Leave

      From April 2025, a new statutory entitlement exists for parents of babies who require neonatal care after birth. If your baby is admitted to hospital for neonatal care within the first 28 days of life and stays for at least 7 consecutive days, you may be entitled to additional neonatal care leave and pay on top of your maternity leave entitlement. This is a separate pot; it does not eat into your maternity leave. Full details and current eligibility criteria are available at gov.uk/neonatal-care-pay-leave.

      Stillbirth

      If your baby is stillborn after the end of the 24th week of pregnancy, you are entitled to the full amount of maternity leave and pay as if your baby had been born alive, under section 15.32 of the NHS Terms and Conditions of Service. Grief does not have a schedule, and you should not feel pressured to return before you are ready. If you need more support, your occupational health department, your educational supervisor, and organisations such as Sands (the stillbirth and neonatal death charity) are available to help.

      ℹ️

      Miscarriage: Before 25 Weeks

      If you suffer a pregnancy loss before 25 weeks, it is classified as a miscarriage under section 15.33 of the NHS Terms and Conditions of Service, and normal sickness absence provisions apply. You are entitled to time off to recover and your employer should treat this with sensitivity. If you need more support than sick leave allows, speak to your occupational health department or educational supervisor. The Miscarriage Association (miscarriageassociation.org.uk) also offers dedicated support.

      🎯 Chapter Summary

      • Preterm births before 29 weeks: you can split your maternity leave so time in the neonatal unit does not eat into your leave at home.
      • Neonatal care leave and pay: a separate statutory entitlement from April 2025 for babies requiring hospital neonatal care of at least 7 consecutive days. Check gov.uk/neonatal-care-pay-leave.
      • Stillbirth after 24 weeks: full maternity leave and pay entitlement applies under section 15.32.
      • Miscarriage before 25 weeks: normal sick leave provisions apply under section 15.33. Support is available through occupational health and the Miscarriage Association.

      Chapter 11

      Returning to Work and Working Flexibly

      The return is often under-planned. It shouldn't be, and it doesn't have to mean going back full-time.

      Notice Requirements

      You must give your employer at least 28 days written notice of your return date, though 8 weeks is safer in practice. The process for rotational trainees works like this: you notify your deanery of your return to work first; they will confirm your next rotation. You then notify the HR coordinator of that new rotation to arrange KIT days, annual leave, and bank holiday dates. If you want to return later than originally planned, you can extend up to 52 weeks total without requiring approval.

      KIT Days (Keeping in Touch)

      You are entitled to up to 10 Keeping in Touch (KIT) days during your maternity leave; days where you can do work without it technically ending your leave or affecting your pay. They are not compulsory, and both you and your employer must agree to each one in advance. They are usually paid at your basic rate for hours worked. KIT days can be valuable for attending mandatory training updates (CPD, ALS), portfolio reviews or ARCPs, or simply easing back in gradually before your formal return.

      ⚠️

      KIT Days Must Be Pre-Arranged

      Never turn up for a KIT day without written prior agreement. Any work done without agreement could be treated as a return to work, ending your maternity leave and cutting your remaining pay. An email confirmation is sufficient, but always get it before attending.

      🧒

      Childcare Costs on KIT Days

      If you work a KIT, SPLiT, or SuppoRTT day, you can apply to your trust for additional funding to cover any childcare costs incurred in order to work. Payment is at the discretion of the trust, but it is worth asking, even if it is not guaranteed.

      Using Annual Leave on Return

      Your best option on return is to use your accrued annual leave before resuming full clinical duties. You accrue annual leave throughout all 52 weeks of maternity leave, including the unpaid period, and you continue to accrue during any annual leave you take immediately on return. After a full year of maternity leave, you could return with 35 to 40 days of paid leave to use. Arrange these with the HR coordinator of your new rotation before your return date.

      🩺 Real-Life Scenario: Maternity Leave, Annual Leave, Then LTFT

      Question: I worked full time before maternity leave, then went on maternity leave and continued to accrue annual leave during that time. After maternity leave ended, I took a period of annual leave immediately afterwards instead of returning straight to work. During that annual leave period, I also continued to accrue further annual leave. I was then planning to return to work at 60% LTFT. Could all of this leave be used on my new 60% rota effectively giving me more time off?

      Answer: No. If your contract remained full time throughout maternity leave and the annual leave taken afterwards, that leave would normally continue on your full-time terms. Annual leave continues to accrue while you are taking annual leave. Your LTFT change only starts on your official return-to-work date; leave accrued before that point is usually still full-time leave. Only leave accrued after the LTFT change moves to the 60% rate.

      Your Right to Return

      After ordinary maternity leave (the first 26 weeks), you have the right to return to the same job. After additional maternity leave (weeks 27–52), you have the right to return to the same or a suitable alternative job on the same or better terms. For rotational trainees, your next training post is determined by your deanery rotation schedule; it is possible your trust or placement will change, but the principle of being treated fairly on return still applies across trusts.

      Considering Less Than Full Time (LTFT) Training on Return

      Returning from maternity leave is one of the most common and well-supported reasons to apply for Less Than Full Time training. LTFT allows you to complete your training at a reduced percentage of full-time hours, typically 50–80% FTE, while completing all the same competencies; it simply takes longer.

      Key things to know if you are considering LTFT on return:

      • Apply at least 16 weeks before you want to start. Applications go through your deanery's PGMDE Support Portal and take time to process; don't leave it late. The moment you know you want LTFT, submit the application and tell your TPD at the same time.
      • Your pay is pro-rata. At 60% LTFT, a £40,000 salary becomes £24,000. On-call supplements are also pro-rata.
      • You can request fixed non-working days for childcare, though these are not guaranteed. Get any agreed pattern confirmed in writing before your placement starts.
      • Your CCT date moves proportionally. Request written confirmation of your new projected CCT date once LTFT is confirmed.
      • You can do locum shifts while LTFT to supplement your income.
      • A £1,000 per year LTFT allowance exists under the 2016 TCS; check your specific contract.
      • If both you and your partner are doctors, consider both going LTFT. This can share the childcare load and allow both careers to progress at a more sustainable pace.
      💡

      Talk to Your Educational Supervisor Early

      Let your educational supervisor know your return plans well in advance. They can help flag any portfolio or competency requirements to complete before leaving, and can support ARCP planning around your return date.

      Supported Return to Training (SuppoRTT)

      NHSE and many deaneries offer SuppoRTT programmes for trainees who have been out for 3 or more months. These can include refresher placements, supernumerary time, coaching, and formal supported return plans. Ask your deanery about SuppoRTT well in advance of your return, as these programmes have limited capacity and require forward planning.

      🩺 Personal Experience: SuppoRTT and the Return

      I personally used my SuppoRTT entitlement. I accessed coaching which helped me enormously in terms of my identity as a working mother, setting boundaries, and feeling confident about returning. I also used supernumerary days at the beginning of my return, which helped ease the transition enormously. If it's available to you, use it.

      🌟

      Be Compassionate With Yourself

      Returning to medicine after maternity leave, especially with a young baby or children, is genuinely hard. Clinical confidence wavers, energy is limited, and the emotional transition is significant. SuppoRTT exists because the profession recognises this. Use every resource available to you. You are not behind; you are returning.

      Childcare: Plan Well Ahead

      This is one of the most practically important things in this guide, and often one of the least discussed. Childcare waiting lists at nurseries can be 1 to 2 years long. If you are planning to use nursery care, join waiting lists as early as possible, ideally in the first trimester. I found out I was pregnant with twins and booked a nursery place when I was just 16 weeks pregnant.

      When you return to work you will be required to do night shifts and on-calls, some of which may fall on your non-working days. If your baby is not in nursery on those days, make sure you have a backup contact: a nanny, babysitter, or trusted friend or family member, whose number you can call. Living without family support nearby with twins on the way meant I had to plan this very carefully.

      Breastfeeding at Work

      When you return to work as a breastfeeding mother, your employer has obligations to you under workplace health and safety regulations. A risk assessment must be carried out to identify any adjustments needed to your working pattern or environment. If your normal working pattern would prevent you from successfully breastfeeding or endanger your health, adjustments must be made; and those adjustments are pay protected. You cannot be penalised financially for needing to express milk or adjust your hours.

      In practical terms, your trust is required to provide:

      • More frequent rest breaks for breastfeeding or expressing
      • A private, hygienic space to express milk (this cannot be a toilet)
      • Somewhere to lie down if needed
      • A fridge to store expressed milk
      💡

      Raise This at Your Pre-Return Meeting

      When you complete your SuppoRTT pre-return form with your educational supervisor, this is the right moment to raise breastfeeding needs. Your supervisor can carry out the risk assessment at that meeting, or refer you to occupational health. Don't wait until your first day back to raise it; by then the rota is set and adjustments are harder to make.

      🎯 Chapter Summary

      • Give 28 days notice of your return, but 8 weeks is safer in practice.
      • Save KIT days for the nil-pay period (months 9–12) for maximum financial benefit.
      • Apply for LTFT at least 16 weeks in advance; tell your TPD at the same time.
      • Use SuppoRTT if available: coaching, supernumerary time, and supported return plans can make a significant difference.
      • Join childcare waiting lists as early as possible; lists can be 1 to 2 years long.
      • Chase payroll at your new trust during unpaid leave to ensure you are re-added before annual leave starts.
      Chapter 12

      Your Partner's Entitlements: New Parent Support Leave and Shared Parental Leave

      Parental leave isn't just for the person who gave birth. Here is what your partner is entitled to, and how you can share the leave between you.

      You can choose to split your maternity leave between you and your partner. This chapter explains how.

      New Parent Support Leave (Paternity Leave)

      New Parent Support Leave is the NHS occupational equivalent of paternity leave. It applies to the father of the child, or the mother's partner or spouse, regardless of sex or gender.

      To qualify, your partner needs 12 months of continuous NHS service by the qualifying week. If they meet this threshold, they are entitled to 2 weeks of fully paid leave (minus the value of statutory paternity pay). The pay amount is calculated in the same way as maternity leave; based on average weekly earnings from the two payslips preceding week 25 of pregnancy.

      ℹ️

      What If They Don't Have 12 Months NHS Service?

      If your partner doesn't yet have 12 months continuous NHS service, they are still entitled to statutory paternity leave; also 2 weeks, paid at 90% of average weekly earnings or the standard statutory rate, whichever is higher. This applies even for multiple births; you only get one period of 2 weeks regardless of how many babies arrive. If your baby is stillborn after 24 weeks, or dies after birth, the entitlement to 2 weeks still remains.

      Shared Parental Leave (SPL): The Basics

      Shared Parental Leave allows you and your partner to split the 52 weeks of parental leave between you, rather than the mother taking all of it as maternity leave. It exists to give families flexibility, and to recognise that many partners now take on an equal or primary caring role.

      To use SPL, the parent requesting it must have 12 months continuous NHS service by the 11th week before the due date. The other parent must meet a statutory employment and earnings test; broadly, they must have been employed or self-employed for 26 out of the 66 weeks preceding the expected week of childbirth, and earned at least £30 a week for 13 of those weeks.

      How SPL Actually Works

      • The total leave available between both parents is 52 weeks, minus any maternity leave already taken.
      • SPL must all be taken within 1 year of the child being born.
      • You can take SPL as a single continuous block or in multiple blocks; however, employers are only obligated to agree to one single continuous block. If you want to take it in multiple discontinuous blocks, the trust has the authority to agree or refuse, and must give a reason for refusal.
      • You can provide up to 3 possible start dates, and can change your intended start date up to 3 times.
      • You must give at least 8 weeks notice before your intended SPL start date.
      💡

      One Block Cannot Be Refused

      If you request one single continuous block of SPL, your employer cannot refuse it. This is worth knowing if you are concerned about flexibility on the trust's part. Plan your single block request carefully, and you have certainty.

      How SPL Pay Works

      • Between both parents, there are 37 weeks of statutory shared parental leave pay (ShPP) available in total.
      • The NHS parent on SPL is entitled to 6 weeks full pay and 18 weeks half pay (this is the occupational element), plus ShPP for the remaining statutory entitlement.
      • The amount of occupational pay available is reduced proportionally based on how much of the 37 statutory weeks the other parent has already claimed.
      • If both parents work for the NHS, the NHS will not pay occupational rates to both simultaneously; one receives occupational pay and the other receives new parent support leave and ShPP.

      A Real Example of How SPL Can Work

      Say the mother takes 19 weeks of maternity leave. That leaves 33 weeks of SPL to share (19 + 33 = 52). Her partner takes 10 weeks of SPL directly after her maternity leave ends. The mother then returns to work while her partner is on leave. The mother then decides to take the remaining 23 weeks of SPL after her partner returns. The partner separately takes 2 weeks of new parent support leave immediately after the birth, which sits outside the SPL pot entirely.

      This kind of arrangement gives both parents extended time at home with the baby while allowing career continuity for both. It is worth planning carefully with HR well in advance, as the paperwork and notice periods need managing.

      ⚠️

      Chase Payroll Before Your Annual Leave Starts

      A common and frustrating problem: HR at the new trust forgets to re-add you to payroll before your accrued annual leave period begins. This means you do not get paid during your annual leave, even though you are entitled to it. Touch base with payroll during the unpaid part of your maternity or SPL to confirm the return to payroll is being set up in time.

      Conclusion

      A final word from the author.

      The NHS is extraordinary. It is also, at times, bewildering to navigate, particularly when you are pregnant, exhausted, and trying to figure out whether the HR advice you just received is actually correct. This guide exists because too many doctors have lost money, missed entitlements, or returned to work before they were ready, simply because no one gave them the full picture.

      What I hope you take from this is not just the facts, but the confidence to use them. To ask the questions. To push back when something does not seem right. To take every week of leave you are entitled to without guilt.

      You have spent years learning to advocate for your patients. This is your permission to advocate for yourself with the same rigour.

      A few things worth remembering as you close this guide:

      • Your entitlements exist in writing. If HR tells you something that contradicts what you have read here, ask them to point you to the specific clause.
      • The BMA is on your side. Their employment advisers are free, confidential, and experienced in exactly these situations. Use them.
      • Timing matters, but life is unpredictable. Do what you can with the information you have, and do not beat yourself up if things do not go to plan.
      • You are not alone. Every doctor who has gone through this has had to piece it together somehow. Hopefully, this guide made it a little easier for you.

      Pregnancy in medicine is not a career setback. It is a chapter; one that comes with its own challenges, its own joys, and, as it turns out, its own surprisingly complex pay structure.

      Being Pregnant at Work

      Do I have to use annual leave for antenatal appointments?

      No. You have a legal right to paid time off for all antenatal care, including recommended classes, without needing to use annual leave. Read more →

      How do I get my work adjusted, for example being taken off nights during pregnancy?

      When you tell your employer you are pregnant, they are legally required to carry out a pregnancy risk assessment. This is how adjustments such as coming off nights or on-calls are arranged. Read more →

      Will my pay be affected if adjustments reduce my working hours?

      No. If your working pattern is adjusted during pregnancy your pay must remain the same. If the trust cannot accommodate suitable alternative work, you must be suspended on full pay. Read more →

      What happens if I am sick during pregnancy?

      Pregnancy-related sickness is treated separately from standard sick leave and will not normally count against your sickness record. The rules change in the final four weeks before your due date. Read more →

      Do I have to rotate to a new trust if I am heavily pregnant?

      No. If you are due to rotate after week 29 of pregnancy, you are entitled to choose to remain at your current trust until you give birth, with your contract extended accordingly. Read more →

      Understanding Your Pay Entitlement

      What is Occupational Maternity Pay (OMP) and how do I qualify?

      OMP is the enhanced NHS maternity pay package. To qualify, you need 12 months of continuous NHS service by around week 29 of pregnancy and must intend to return to NHS work for at least 13 weeks. Read more →

      What is Statutory Maternity Pay (SMP) and how do I qualify?

      SMP is the government-funded statutory minimum, paid for up to 39 weeks. It requires 26 weeks of continuous service with the same employer by the qualifying week. Rotational trainees have additional protections. Read more →

      How is my due date calculated and why does it matter?

      Your official due date is confirmed at your 12-week scan and determines your qualifying week and all key pay dates. Even a few days' difference can shift important deadlines. Read more →

      I rotate trusts: does that mean I miss out on SMP?

      Not necessarily. Trainee protections mean your current employer may be required to pay the SMP equivalent even if you changed trust during training. Check your lead employer arrangement early. Read more →

      What if I don't qualify for OMP or SMP?

      You may still be entitled to Maternity Allowance, a government benefit paid through HMRC. Unlike SMP, it is non-taxable, and it is available even with a limited employment history. Read more →

      What are the key dates and weeks for qualifying for OMP and SMP?

      OMP eligibility is assessed at week 29 of pregnancy; SMP eligibility at week 25. Your SMP pay rate is based on earnings in the 8-week window ending around week 25. Read more →

      Can I take a break from the NHS and still keep continuous service?

      Yes, in many cases. Gaps of up to 3 months, approved career breaks, overseas training, and previous parental leave can all preserve continuity. The rules are broader than most doctors realise. Read more →

      I was on sick leave during the SMP calculation window: what happens to my pay calculation?

      Under section 15.23 of the NHS Terms and Conditions, your SMP should be calculated on your notional full sick pay for that period, not on reduced earnings. Raise this with HR if it applies to you. Read more →

      My qualifying week spanned two trusts: will both payslips count?

      Yes. Both payslips and any annual leave payments from your previous trust should be included in your SMP calculation. Flag this explicitly with HR at your new trust. Read more →

      My fixed-term contract ends before or during maternity leave: what happens?

      If you have already triggered maternity entitlement while employed, your contract is normally extended for maternity purposes. The BMA also confirms that if the contract would have ended regardless of pregnancy, OMP repayment provisions do not apply. Read more →
